# Rover — AFL Masters Development Guide

## Role Summary

The Rover is a small, agile midfielder who works around the stoppages, feeding off the ruck contest to win and distribute the ball at ground level. You are the link between the contest and your forwards, thriving on quick hands, sharp reading of the ball, and clean disposal under pressure. As an over-35 amateur, your value comes from positioning and anticipation rather than raw speed.

## Positioning & Movement

- Stay low and balanced at stoppages, ready to pounce on the ruck tap rather than standing flat-footed.
- Position on the drop side of the ruck contest, anticipating where the tap or crumb will fall.
- Hover a metre or two off the contest so you can react and accelerate onto the ball.
- After winning the ball, move it forward quickly and reset into space to offer again.

## Core Skills & Drills

### 1. Ground-Ball Pickups
Most Rover possessions come off the deck, so clean hands at pace are essential.
- Roll a ball and scoop it cleanly while jogging; progress to picking up and handballing in one motion.

### 2. Quick Handball Under Pressure
Your job is to release the ball fast before tacklers arrive.
- Two metres apart, handball rapidly off both hands; add a third person applying light pressure.

### 3. Snap Shooting for Goal
Rovers crumb forward stoppages and need to convert from awkward angles.
- Drop the ball and snap from 25-30m at varying angles.

### 4. Reading the Ruck Tap
Winning first possession depends on anticipating the tap direction.
- Partner palms a thrown ball in different directions; you react, gather, and call the side early.

## Common Mistakes

- Standing too upright at stoppages, slowing your reaction to the ball.
- Diving into packs and getting buried instead of staying mobile outside.
- Trying to break the lines with speed you no longer have, rather than a quick handball.
- Holding the ball too long and getting caught.

## Smart Conditioning (Over-35s)

- Dynamic warm-up (leg swings, lunges, build-up runs) before any contest work.
- Repeat-effort fitness with 10-15 second bursts and full recovery, not long runs.
- Hamstring, glute, and core work twice a week to reduce soft-tissue risk.
- Respect recovery; leave niggles alone and prioritise sleep and hydration.

## What Good Looks Like

- You consistently win first hands at the contest by reading the ruck.
- Your disposal is quick and clean, releasing teammates before pressure arrives.
- You reset into space, offer an option, and chime in for the occasional snap.
