# Ruck Rover — AFL Masters Development Guide

## Role Summary

The ruck rover is one of three midfield "follower" roles, working alongside the rover and ruckman to win and distribute the ball around stoppages. You read where the ruckman will tap and pounce on the crumbs, linking the contest to your runners. Think of yourself as a slightly bigger, more contested-ball version of the rover: comfortable in tight traffic but able to break away and run. The appeal for an over-35 is that smart positioning and anticipation matter more than raw speed.

## Positioning & Movement

- Set up a step or two off the ruck contest, on the side your ruckman tends to tap toward.
- Stay light on your feet and keep moving in small arcs around the stoppage.
- After winning or feeding the ball, push to open space as an outlet, then recover to the next contest.
- Communicate with your ruckman before each stoppage so you both know where the tap is going.

## Core Skills & Drills

### 1. Reading the Tap (Anticipation)
Getting first hands on the ball is what makes a ruck rover valuable.
- Partner ruckman calls their tap direction, you react and gather; progress to no call.

### 2. Clean Ground-Ball Pickups
Most touches come off the deck in traffic.
- Roll or bounce a ball and pick it up at walking then jogging pace; add light pressure.

### 3. Quick Handball Under Pressure
You often release to a runner instantly.
- Handball against a wall or partner off both sides, then add a defender chasing.

### 4. Short, Accurate Kicking
Your job is to link play cleanly, not bomb it long.
- 15-20m passes to a partner leading at you, chest height, preferred side.

## Common Mistakes

- Crashing straight into the contest instead of positioning for the crumb.
- Over-handballing into traffic under no real pressure.
- Forgetting to follow up after a feed, so you stop being an outlet.
- Ball-watching rather than reading the likely tap and your opponent.

## Smart Conditioning (Over-35s)

- Warm up hips, groin and hamstrings before contested work.
- Build calf and Achilles with simple raises twice a week.
- Limit full-contact stoppage reps in any single session.
- Prioritise recovery: light mobility, hydration and sleep over extra hard running.

## What Good Looks Like

- You're consistently first or second to the loose ball at stoppages.
- Your disposals reliably hit a teammate, keeping the ball moving forward.
- You stay involved after the contest, linking midfield to forwards.
